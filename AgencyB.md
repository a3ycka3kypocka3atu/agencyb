When dealing with traditional, practical business owners, the golden rule is: **show them the money, the time saved, and the predictability.** They don't want to fund a science experiment; they want to buy a reliable system that generates results.  
Here is how I recommend presenting pricing and case studies to this specific audience:

### **1\. Presenting Pricing: Predictability is Key**

Traditional business owners hate open-ended budgets, vague estimates, and hidden fees. They want to know exactly what it costs and what they get.

* **Fixed Packages over Hourly Rates:** Give them clear, neatly boxed packages (e.g., "Complete Lead Generation Funnel: $X"). This makes your service feel like a tangible, safe product they are buying off a shelf.  
* **Tie Cost to ROI:** Frame the pricing around how quickly they will make it back. Position it as a direct investment: *"This CRM automation saves your sales team 20 hours a week. At your current payroll, this system pays for itself in 3 months."*  
* **Phased Milestones:** If it's a large project, break the pricing into logical, business-focused phases (e.g., Phase 1: Audit & Strategy, Phase 2: Build, Phase 3: Launch). It drastically reduces their perceived financial risk.

### **2\. Presenting Case Studies: The "Before & After" Method**

They do not care that you used React, Supabase, or n8n workflows. They care about the business impact. Structure your case studies strictly around the **Problem ➔ Solution ➔ Result** framework.

* **Highlight the Hard Metrics:** Use bold numbers at the very top. *"Increased lead conversion by 35%,"* *"Cut manual data entry by 40 hours a month,"* or *"Generated $50k in new sales within 60 days."*  
* **Focus on the "Before":** Describe a painful, inefficient situation that the prospect can instantly relate to (e.g., *"They were losing 50% of their leads because their sales team couldn't follow up fast enough"*).  
* **Keep the "How" Brief:** Briefly mention *what* you built (e.g., *"We implemented an automated lead-routing system"*), but completely skip the technical jargon.
